My reflections on AI the past 12+ months? It's been a pretty unique period for me with a couple of different jobs, some time off work lots of change. At the same time the tech world has seen huge change with AI at the minimium being a huge converstaion topic and at the other end taking over from entire teams. My timeline has meant I've perhaps been more open to trying out new tools and experimenting. It's now a regular part of day to day life - but I can still imagine a different path (and probably one that many might be on) where I'd still be struggling to make the most of the new opportunties and feeling a bit bemused by what seems like the huge progress and productivity that many are shouting about.

Here's some of my thoughts on the past 12 months. I think the main reflection is that, like learning anything, if you have some happy mix of time, motivation and resources then you'll do much better. Some of this comes from within you, some is enviromental.

No time, no money, poor results

My starting point a year ago was at a small startup. Lots of big ideas but little extra time or moeny. AI surely would help - however the models and tools back then were either constrained to simple autocompletion (and wow was that amazing when copilot auto compelted a full reduce function for the first time!) or were complicated tooling sets or expensive. We used what we could but a step change would involve both time and money without clear obvious results.

Time off / reset

In early 20205 I had a few months out of the tech world - dealing with the v analgue world of nappies, naps and nurturing. We all know a reset is good and I think this chunk of time away from computers was good for me. It also conincided with big developments in the AI world. Agents were improving, claude codde got introduced in Feb and rolled out officially at the same time I started a new role.

Time, money, good results

May 2025 I started a new job. When you've been at a place for a while you have a sense of everything, you're in control - especially when part of a small team and codebase. Starting somewhere new means those certainties aren't there. There was also encouragement to use AI, budget to do so and also new tools and agents. This meant I embraced claude code more than perhaps I would of done in my previous role. It also fitted into my workflow - I have spent a long time optimising my workflow for the terminal - claude code fitted right in with that unlike other tools which required a bigger shift in ways of working. Claude sat along nicely beng a complement to my day to day (although little did I know almost a year later I'd shift mostly out of the code editor and mainly be in claude!).

Being somewhere new the AI was super helpful at researching the code base, helping me iterate and improve. It wasn't plain sailing - writing claude.md files felt odd - who knows if they work? the agents could be super persuasive and very wrong. But like with most tools using it more and more you got a sense of where it would work or not. For example we wanted to generate some test data - the argument was that the AI would hallucinate ids etc. having played with it a bit the better route was to prompt it to create a script to generate the data - this could be clearly rationalised and rerun. It's a pattern I've used regaularly - but if I hadn't had some experience playing around I might of pushed it to generate some data and then been frustrracted if it wasn't repeatable or properly structured.

Headspace

Time and budget were super helpful (was nice to be at a place where money wasn't so tight - accidenteally spending $500 on agents one month wasn't an issue). But I also think a big change was shifting from a role I'd had for about 8 years - one of contributing code but also of managing a team. I love working with others and helping them progress - it does take up a lot of time and energy. Shifting to an IC role gave me a huge amount of free headspace to try things out, think, research etc.

As the agents have progressed and got smarter some of the skills I learnt as a manager came into affect. If you're used to managing a team you're used to things happening that one step removed - you know what's going on but maybe not all the detail. You guide the team and nudge them. You're used to things sometiems going in the wrong direction, having to change direction, learning from mistakes. You have to be good a top level design and plans. Be able to get a sense of how things are doing, making decisions on when to jump in, when to add detail or to take over, when to step back and let things run. having lots of tasks runnign in parrallel - different projects/tasks going on at the same time.

Another change - more structure / better models

A second new job- this one there was a huge remit and a v small team. But there again was the support in terms of time - budget not quite as free. There was also a v strong code base, lots of test. Things evolving rather than rapidly evolving. There was also v clear structure of very small PRs and commits. Having this rigour on top of the AI skilsl I learnt was a huge boost. At the same time the models were getting better, we wrote better MD files, I got better at using the tools. So much change it's hard to pin down the one thing but now I live mostly in claude code - but extra time is spent splitting out the work, explaining it, testing it and getting rigurours reviews. Am much more confident

The other path

I can imagine a path where i was working on the same codebase as a year ago - with tight time and money plus a role managing people. Am sure I'd be using AI still but I don't think I'd of had the time or headspace or the challenge of tackling a new codebase to progress as quickly. I can see how people are getting huge productivity out of AI but can imagine if this other path where I'd of been fairly disbelievign of some of the claims of boosts in speed. There must be plenty of people out there who have dabbled but haven't had those opportunities to learn.

how to learn

like any skill it takes time. AI is doing the work you did before but maybe in a different way. The more you play with it you get a sense of how to guide it, where it works well, where it doesn't. You setup guardrails, plans, ideas, ways of working. But that all takes time, some money and the space to be able to experiement, to go slower before you go faster. Maybe that means psuhing for that space at work, maybe it's sacrificing some of your own time or money. Or maybe you can wait. It's all changing so much - things that were useful or standard practice a year ago don't make any sesne now. You don't need to be first but you probalby don't want to be last. And maybe it's all hype - but a tool which I use 90% of the day I don't think can be my imgaination. I know am doing good work - It's defitely not slower - i think it's a decent cunk faster and it's allowed me to also be productive v quickly in new languages or frameworks.
